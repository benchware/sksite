The Basic Laws of the Separated Kingdom

Provisional Code of the Federation — in force pending ratification of the Constitution.

Status: In force pending ratification. These Basic Laws are the supreme law of the Federation until a permanent Constitution is ratified.

Preamble

Whereas the member states of the Separated Kingdom have until now existed without a common code, governed only by custom, goodwill, and the inconsistent mercy of whichever armed authority happened to be nearest;

And whereas this absence of law has produced disputes over land, property, citizenship, trade, public conduct, and the proper limits of authority that goodwill alone cannot resolve;

The Separated Kingdom Supreme Court hereby enacts these Basic Laws as the supreme law of the Federation until such time as a permanent Constitution is drafted, ratified, and brought into force. Upon ratification, the Constitution shall supersede this code, except where it expressly preserves provisions herein.

Title I — Fundamental Rights and Liberties

Art. 1 — Right to Life

No citizen or resident may be killed except in lawful combat as defined under Title IV, or by due process of law. Killing by deception, ambush, or killing of an unarmed friendly citizen is unlawful regardless of any other provision.

Art. 2 — Right to Property

A citizen's land, builds, and stored items are inviolable. No person, including state or federal officials, may seize, destroy, or alter them without consent, a valid warrant, or lawful eminent domain proceedings under Title V.

Art. 3 — Freedom of Movement

Citizens may travel and relocate freely between member states. No state may wall off, toll, or trap travellers at its borders except for clearly marked toll zones disclosed in advance.

Art. 4 — Freedom of Speech, Press, and Petition

Citizens may speak, publish, and petition the government without fear of arrest, provided such speech does not constitute fraud, incitement to unlawful violence, targeted harassment, or disclosure of a person's real-world identity.

Art. 5 — Freedom of Association

Citizens may form guilds, parties, companies, associations, and unions freely. The Federation may not ban a political party solely for its platform.

Art. 6 — Due Process

No citizen shall be excluded, exiled, fined, or stripped of property except by a process that includes notice of the accusation, an opportunity to respond, and a ruling by a neutral party under Title VII. Summary administrative punishment without process is unlawful.

Art. 7 — Equality Before the Law

All citizens are equal before federal law regardless of member state, wealth, seniority of residence, office, faction, or social status.

Art. 8 — Right to Vote and Stand for Office

Every citizen of voting age under Article 12 may vote in federal and state elections and may stand for any office for which they are qualified.

Art. 9 — Right to Bear Tools of Trade

Citizens may craft, carry, and trade weapons and tools for lawful self-defence, hunting, and labour, subject only to published restrictions on banned ordnance under Article 34.

Art. 10 — Prohibition on Cruel Punishment

Permanent exclusion, total confiscation of possessions, unlawful confinement, or demolition of builds may never be imposed as a first penalty. Restitution and proportionate fines come first. Banishment is a last resort reserved for repeat or severe offences under Title IV.

Title II — Citizenship

Art. 11 — Federal Citizenship

Citizenship of the Federation is held automatically by citizens of any member state in good standing. A citizen of one state is, by that fact, a citizen of the Federation, and may not be treated as a foreigner in any other member state.

Art. 12 — Residency and Voting Citizenship

A new entrant becomes a Resident upon first registration in the Kingdom, and becomes a full Citizen with voting rights after approval by member citizens and a period without a serious offence on record.

Art. 13 — Dual Citizenship and Office

Dual citizenship across member states is permitted. A citizen may hold land in more than one state, but may cast a federal vote only once per election and may serve in only one local office at any given period of time.

Art. 14 — Protection Against Loss of Citizenship

Citizenship may not be revoked as a punishment. Exile from a state does not strip federal citizenship; an exiled citizen may resettle in any other member state.

Title III — The Federal Structure

Art. 15 — State Authority

Each member state retains full authority over its internal affairs, including local ordinances, zoning, local taxation, and town government, except in matters reserved to the Federation under Article 16.

Art. 16 — Reserved Federal Powers

Only the Federation may mint or recognize official currency; conduct diplomacy, treaties, or war with outside nations; maintain shared federal infrastructure; regulate interstate trade routes and disputes between two or more states; and amend this Basic Law or ratify the Constitution.

Art. 17 — Supremacy of Federal Law

No state law may contradict this Basic Law. Where a conflict exists, federal law prevails. States may provide stronger rights protections, but never weaker ones.

Art. 18 — Secession

Any state may petition to secede. Secession requires a referendum of that state's citizens followed by federal parliamentary review, not unilateral declaration.

Art. 19 — Admission of New States

New territories or settlements may petition for admission as a member state once they meet minimum criteria for population, functioning local government, and resolution of outstanding claim disputes as set by federal statute.

Title IV — Criminal Code

Art. 20 — Griefing and Destructive Damage

Unauthorized destruction, modification, or theft from another person's land is destructive damage. Penalty: full restitution by rebuilding or compensation, plus a fine. Repeat offences may escalate to temporary banishment.

Art. 21 — Theft

Taking items, blocks, livestock, currency, documents, or goods belonging to another without consent is theft. Penalty: return of goods plus damages; habitual theft escalates under Article 20.

Art. 22 — Unlawful Killing

Killing another citizen outside lawful combat under Article 23 is unlawful. Penalty is scaled by intent and harm, from fines to temporary banishment for premeditated or repeated killings.

Art. 23 — Lawful Combat

Killing is lawful only when all parties accept combat; during a formally declared war between states or factions against combatants; in self-defence against an unlawful attacker; or in a sanctioned duel with witnessed consent from both parties.

Art. 24 — Slavery and Coercion Prohibited

No person may be forced to labour, traded as property, or imprisoned without due process. Indentured debt bondage of any kind is void and unenforceable, regardless of any private contract claiming otherwise.

Art. 25 — Fraud

Deliberate deception in trade, including false advertising of items, counterfeit or duplicated goods, and bait-and-switch deals, is fraud. Penalty: restitution plus a fine proportional to the value defrauded.

Art. 26 — Treason

Sabotage of federal infrastructure, betrayal during declared federal defence, or deliberate disabling of shared public systems constitutes treason and is subject to the severest lawful penalties under Article 28.

Art. 27 — Trespass and Border Offences

Entering private land or restricted federal property without authorization is trespass. It is a minor offence unless accompanied by theft, destructive damage, evasion of lawful inspection, or violence.

Art. 28 — Sentencing Principles

All penalties must be proportionate to harm done, restitution-first where possible, appealable to a higher court, and never imposed by a single official acting alone in cases warranting more than a minor fine.

Art. 29 — Defences

Self-defence, lack of intent, mistake of fact, necessity, and entrapment are valid defences to any charge under this Title.

Title V — Property and Land Law

Art. 30 — Acquisition of Land

Land becomes a citizen's property upon lawful acquisition from a previous owner, valid grant, public auction, inheritance, or registry entry under local law.

Art. 31 — Abandoned Land

Land left unbuilt and untouched for 30 days without owner activity, economic use, maintenance, or construction may be declared abandoned and returned to the Commons after prior public notice. The owner may appeal before the period ends to extend the protection period.

Art. 32 — Eminent Domain

The Federation or a state may reclaim private land for public works, including roads, transit corridors, and federal hubs, only with public vote or council approval and fair compensation in currency or equivalent land agreed before construction begins.

Art. 33 — Inheritance

Land passes by inheritance to a named heir upon a citizen's permanent departure. If no heir is named, claims revert to the Commons after the abandonment period.

Art. 34 — Contraband and Restricted Materials

Explosives, firearms, destructive fluids, offensive incendiary materials, and other items designated as restricted by a state or the Federation may not be stockpiled or transported across state lines without a permit. The restricted list must be published publicly and may not be enforced retroactively.

Title VI — Economic Law

Art. 35 — Currency

The Federation shall designate or mint an official currency recognized in all member states, being the Euro unless changed by federal statute. States may use local scrip for internal trade, but federal currency must be accepted at federal markets and for federal taxes.

Art. 36 — Free Trade

No member state may impose tariffs or border tolls on goods travelling between member states. Trade between citizens of different states is to be treated identically to trade within a single state.

Art. 37 — Taxation

States may levy local taxes for local services. The Federation may levy a federal tax only by act of the federal legislature, with revenue spent on shared infrastructure, defence, or judicial administration, and never diverted to private use by officials.

Art. 38 — Public Markets

Public markets and auction houses operated by the Federation must be open to all citizens on equal terms.

Title VII — Government, Elections, and Judiciary

Art. 39 — Legislature

A federal council or parliament, with representatives from each member state, holds the power to pass federal statutes, approve budgets, and propose amendments to this Basic Law.

Art. 40 — Executive

A President enforces federal law and represents the Federation in dealings with outside nations, but may not unilaterally create criminal penalties or seize property. Such actions require legislative or judicial authority.

Art. 41 — Elections

Federal elections occur at fixed intervals set by statute. All citizens under Article 12 may vote, and any citizen may stand for office unless disqualified by conviction for treason or fraud against the Federation.

Art. 42 — Judiciary

Each state maintains a local tribunal for minor disputes and offences. A Federal Court hears appeals from state tribunals, disputes between citizens of different states, treason charges, and constitutional questions.

Art. 43 — Trial Rights

An accused citizen has the right to know the charge, present a defence, call witnesses, receive reasons for judgment, and appeal an adverse ruling once to a higher court.

Art. 44 — Limits on Administrative Power

Administrators and technical officers acting in an official capacity are bound by this Basic Law. Use of administrative powers or emergency technical controls to punish, seize, confine, or kill outside a lawful ruling violates Article 6 and Article 2.

Title VIII — Transition, Supremacy, and Amendment

Art. 45 — Transitional Supremacy

This Basic Law is the supreme law of the Federation from the date of adoption until a permanent Constitution is ratified.

Art. 46 — Relation to the Constitution

The forthcoming Constitution may revise, replace, or entrench any provision with the exception of Title I. Where the Constitution is silent on a matter addressed here, this Basic Law continues in force as ordinary federal statute.

Art. 47 — Amendment Before Ratification

Prior to ratification of the Constitution, this Basic Law may be amended by a supermajority vote of the federal legislature, with notice given to all member states at least one week before the vote.

Art. 48 — Entrenched Rights

Nothing in this Basic Law may be amended in a way that eliminates the rights in Title I without a direct referendum of all citizens.
